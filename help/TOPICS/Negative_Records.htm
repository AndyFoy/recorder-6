Negative records

It can be extremely valuable to record the fact that a particular taxon was sought and NOT found. However, 'negative records' usually have to be treated with a great deal of caution. It is pertinent to ask:

  • were the recorders who made the search experienced in finding the particular taxon or biotope?

  • did they look in the right place?

  • were the correct search methods used?

  • were the conditions, time of year, time of day, etc. correct?

  • how often, for how long, and how persistently was the search carried out (especially relevant if the object of the search is cryptic, secretive or otherwise hard to find)?

It is therefore particularly important to include as much detailed information as possible when making a negative record, so that users of the record can assess how much confidence they can have in it.

Negative records from monitoring programmes tend to be far less controversial. If the same sampling method is applied at regular intervals at the same location, or at a series of locations, to record particular taxa, then zero results in some of the samples can usually be analysed with confidence. This may be as important to an analysis as positive results.

Negative records can be made by entering a measurement with a measurement type of 'Abundance' datum value of zero in a taxon occurrence.

TIP: Negative records will not appear in distribution maps and reports unless you specifically indicate that they should be included (see Report Wizard – Additional filters –Constraints).
