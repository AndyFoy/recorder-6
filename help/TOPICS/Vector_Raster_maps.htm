Map file formats, vectors and rasters

A vector map consists of lines defined by a series of co-ordinates. This is how digitised site boundaries are stored. Their advantages are that they can be stored in a fairly compact format and can be drawn at a wide range of scales without distortion or loss of detail. However, digitising a map is quite a long and labour intensive process. The co-ordinates of each twist and turn in the line that is being digitised have to be read off and recorded electronically. Consequently, vector datasets tend to be expensive and each set of data usually covers only one type of feature (e.g. the boundary of a site).

A raster tile is a picture consisting of rows and columns of coloured pixels. They are made by scanning a paper-map, a satellite image or an aerial photograph. Large images (such as a map sheet covering a sizeable area) are broken down into a set of 'tiles' – rectangular pieces of manageable size – which can be shown side by side on screen to form the complete picture. Given the spatial co-ordinates of the corners of a tile, the co-ordinate of any given pixel can be accurately determined. The advantage of raster maps is that they can contain a lot of information – they can allow a conventional published map or an aerial photograph to be placed on screen. The main disadvantage is that a raster map can only be displayed at a limited range of scales around that at which it was scanned. If you try to display a it at a much smaller scale, then detail is rapidly lost and if you zoom in to a much larger scale then the screen image breaks down into a series of square blocks representing the individual pixels of the original scanned image.

With the ready availability of cheap, desk-top scanners it is easy to scan a paper-map or an aerial photograph. However, the process of making it into useable raster tiles is quite involved and has to be done very carefully if you are going to rely on the result to derive accurate spatial references:

  • When aerial photographs are taken, it is rare for the camera to be pointing absolutely vertically downwards, and this introduces distortions due to perspective. Also, the ground is not flat, and features on slopes will appear closer together or further apart depending on whether the slope faces towards or away from the camera. These distortions have to be corrected in the final image.

  • The rows and columns of pixels in the scanned image must be accurately aligned to the spatial co-ordinate system. Any rotation, or distortion due to variation in the scanning speed across the image, will lead to inaccuracies in the spatial co-ordinates derived from the map tile.

  • The edges of tiles have to be matched up accurately so that tiles fit together properly and features like roads and rivers run from one tile to the next without breaks at the tile boundaries.

  • Ideally, the whole process should be carried out so that the colours of features are consistent across the whole set of tiles. Then similar features can be recognised in different places on the resulting map irrespective of the tile on which they appear.

To do these jobs well requires high quality scanning equipment which can handle large originals (e.g. whole map sheets), and sophisticated software that can correct for distortions in the image and align it to the spatial co-ordinate system (ortho-rectification). There are many commercial agencies that offer these services.

The copyright conditions on published maps usually do not allow their scanning and storage in electronic form. This is the case, for example, for Ordnance Survey maps.
